#About the project

Technological developments in the 21st century have transformed how we interact with the world and each other, and social media platforms have been at the forefront of this process. The centrality of social media has reformed how we as humans connect and communicate; important life events have been reduced to likable photos, heart-felt messages have been replaced with captions, and thoughts and emotions have been compressed into hashtags. The following statistics speak to these realities: about 45% of the population constitutes as social media users; people spend 2 hours and 25 minutes using social media per day(smartinsights.com); 500 million tweets are sent out every day, on average (brandwatch.com). These statistics make clear that social media plays a very central and fundamental role in today’s society.

With this in mind, we are interested in digging deeper into how social media usage might be associated with happiness levels, but more specifically, how people express their emotions using social media.

To look into this further, we have chosen Twitter as the social media platform of focus. We have designed a project aimed at finding out if there is a correlation between happinesslevels of different US cities and Twitter usage of these cities that is specific to emotionally-charged Tweets.

For this project we have utilized two datasets, one which includes Twitter data that we compiled ourselves and one with data that includes happiness indicators and scores of U.S. cities from Wallethub. The former was collected using Twarc. In Twarc, we completed various queries for each city included in our Wallethub dataset. We searched for tweets reflective of differing happiness levels. For higher levels of happiness we searched for tweets that included the words “happy” and “love.” For more neutral levels of happiness we searched for tweets that included the words “okay” and “alright.” For lower levels of happiness we searched for tweets that included the words “sad” and “hate.” We chose these words because we felt that they each correlated to differing levels of emotion. We compiled all of this information into a larger dataset that recorded the number that each word was found in tweets from all the included cities.

The latter dataset was compiled using various already existing reports with measures reflective of the following three categories: (1) emotion and physical well-being, (2) income and employment, and (3) community and environment. Example measures include happiness/depression indexes and suicide rates, poverty rates and income information, and hate-crime data, respectively. The dates of data used ranges from 2010 to 2014.

By mapping botched datasets, we hope to see what kinds of content is being tweeted for each city with a happiness score.
