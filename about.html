#About the project

Technological developments in the 21st century have transformed how we interact with the world and each other, and social media platforms have been at the forefront of this process. The centrality of social media has reformed how we as humans connect and communicate; important life events have been reduced to likable photos, heart-felt messages have been replaced with captions, and thoughts and emotions have been compressed into hashtags. The following statistics speak to these realities: about 45% of the population constitutes as social media users; people spend 2 hours and 25 minutes using social media per day (smartinsights.com); 500 million tweets are sent out every day, on average (brandwatch.com). These statistics make clear that social media plays a very central and fundamental role in today’s society.

With this in mind, the question then is: how does social media usage affect members of society, mentally? We think that this question is especially prevalent now more than ever, as more and more of our time is spent on screens due to the Covid-19 pandemic. Our goal of this project is to dig deeper into this question.

In order to do so, we have chosen two datasets, one with twitter data that includes user location and tweet content information, and one with data that includes happiness indicators and scores of U.S. cities from Wallethub. The former was compiled by the researchers Z. Cheng, J. Caverlee, and K. Lee, who scraped Twitter content from September 2009 to January 2010. The latter was compiled using various already existing reports with measures reflective of the following three categories: (1) emotion and physical well-being, (2) income and employment, and (3) community and environment. Example measures include happiness/depression indexes and suicide rates, poverty rates and income information, and hate-crime data, respectively. The dates of data used ranges from 2010 to 2014.

By mapping both datasets, we hope to see if there is a correlation between the cities with lower happiness scores and with greater Twitter usage. (Note that while these time frames don’t match precisely with the dates of the Twitter data, both datasets will still give us a general idea of this correlation). We are trying to answer our original question by seeing if the cities that have more Twitter users have lower happiness scores and indexes. Ultimately, this project is for anyone who utilizes social media on a daily basis. We want to bring attention to the potential negative effects of using these platforms so that, hopefully, people can make educated decisions about how to spend their time in more constructive ways.
