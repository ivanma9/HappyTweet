#Thoughts

As we have mentioned, social media is an extremely central part of today’s world. We were initially interested in how this can impact people’s mental states after learning more about the various studies that have suggested that social media might impact people’s lives negatively. A Times article cites a 2017 study by researchers Vannuci et al. that found that increased time on social media was connected to higher levels of anxiety (Heid). The same article also brings up the idea of “technology overload” (Heid). More time spent on our phones on these social media platforms are thought to not be very healthy for your brain. Thus, there is some basis for the idea that social media can negatively affect people’s mental states, which justifies our project’s aim to explore how emotions might be reflected in social media.

#Inspiration

The structure of this project was inspired by other researchers who have done Twitter analysis as well. A common project approach we discovered in our research was one that centered around sentiment analysis. These project designs, such as the instructions offered here, used different methods to collect tweets and assign different numerical scores to them, depending on if they were more positive, negative, or neutral. We applied this idea by searching for tweets in Twarc that were more positive (tweets with the words “happy” and “love” in them), neutral (tweets with the words “okay” and “alright” in them), and negative (tweets with the words “sad” and”hate” in them).

This Word Mapper project as well as this also inspired us to use Twarc to create our own dataset and locate our own tweets. These projects allow you to type in any word and see where tweets relating to that term have been tweeted. By following this same idea and using Twarc, we were able to incorporate data that takes into account the content and frequency of tweets.

#Findings

In general, we were able to find more positive-leveled tweets (tweets with the word “happy” and tweets with the word “love”). This could be because the location in which we completed the Twarc searches were for cities with higher happiness scores. This would suggest that social media might in some way be reflective of emotional states, have some correlation to general happiness/emotions. This is a great sign because in 2020 there were several instances of the saddest periods ever recorded on twitter. The coronavirus outbreak combined with civil unrest events such as the killing of Geore Floyd and subsequent Black Lives Matter movement resulted in a record decline in mood across the US. Seeing these elevated numbers is an indication that positive moods are rising.
